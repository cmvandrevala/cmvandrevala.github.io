Background

Each homework problem in the Computerized Interactive Teaching Assistant (CITA) system consists of one "top level" graded question, one or more optional followup questions, and a set of optional tutorial questions with a specific structure (i.e. step-by-step tutorial, single hint tutorial, tutorial with multiple paths of analysis, etc.). The problems are built to highlight Polya's problem solving strategy of (1) understanding the problem, (2) devising a plan, (3) carrying out the plan, and (4) reviewing your work. Each question in a tutorial is constructed to be a step in the analysis of the homework problem and takes about 30 seconds to 1 minute to complete. This might include completing a line of algebra, identifying an assumption in the problem, or selecting a diagram for future analysis. The number of questions (steps) in a tutorial is dependent on the complexity of the "top level" graded question; a simple lens problem might require a tutorial with a dozen steps (6-12 minutes to completion) while a derivation of electric field with Gauss's law might require fifty or more steps (over 25 or more minutes to completion).

Students need to answer the "top level" question to receive credit on the homework problem but can answer the followup and tutorial questions if and when they need help. As students answer the different questions at each level in our homework system, we collect detiled information on when they click the submission button and what they answered. It is important to note that click statistics do not necessarily correspond to time spent on a homework problem. We cannot determine the exact amount of time that students spend on a particular homework problem (tutorials and all) by clicks alone. What if a student opened up a tutorial question and then left the computer to make a sandwich? What if a student re-opened a question before an exam to study? We can, however, analyze the quantity of clicks as a function of time as well as the pattern of clicks between homework problems to learn about how students are using the homework system.

Method

Once we collect the click information for students in a given semester, we bin the responses by a number of parameters including time, student, type of question answered, and whether it was a correct or incorrect answer. We experimented with a number of binning schemes and found that a bin size of twelve hours gave us good information on the general trends of the class while a bin size of two hours allowed us to see detailed patterns in the data without using too much computational power.

From this binned data, we can calculate a variety of descriptive statistics about student's usage of the system throughout the semester. Of course, these statistics are compared with other sources of data including class scores, online surveys, and focus group interview transcripts in order to paint a full picture of what is happening over the course of the semester.

Results and Discussion

Below are three animations that show the click statistics in the first unit of the fall 2015 semester of PHYS 24100 (i.e. approximately five weeks of the semester up until the first exam). We have divided up the clicks into twelve hour bins and used similar categories to those presented on the poster (open problem without answering question, answer top-level question, and answer tutorial question). Followup question clicks, while present, represented a small fraction of the total clicks, and thus were not included in this particular animation due to the scale of the y-axis. The x-axis of each graph gives the problem number in order that they were presented during the semester. The y-axis gives the number of clicks on a problem.

A few key things to note about this data:

  1. The cycles of peaks described on the poster are very easy to see in the animations. Some students might be viewing the problems before the assignment is due, but the vast majority of students focus on a homework assignment in the day or two beforehand.
  2. The number of clicks that opened a problem but did nothing else was higher than expected.
  3. Followup questions were answered in the first unit, but they represented a very small portion of the click activity per day. Students are skipping the optional followup questions.
  4. There is a flurry of click activity before the first exam due to students studying past problems. Analysis shows that tutorials are popular study tools before an exam.

Conclusions and Further Work
