This webpage will expand on what you saw in our poster, diving deeper into our analysis techniques and results.

Background

Each homework problem in the Computerized Interactive Teaching Assistant (CITA) system consists of one "top level" graded question, one or more optional followup questions, and a set of optional tutorial questions with a specific structure (i.e. step-by-step tutorial, single hint tutorial, tutorial with multiple paths of analysis, etc.). The problems are built to highlight Polya's problem solving strategy of (1) understanding the problem, (2) devising a plan, (3) carrying out the plan, and (4) reviewing your work. Each question in a tutorial is designed to be a step in the analysis of the homework problem and takes about 30 seconds to 1 minute to complete. This might include completing a line of algebra, identifying an assumption in the solution, or selecting a diagram for future analysis. The number of questions (steps) in a tutorial is dependent on the complexity of the "top level" graded question; a simple lens problem might require a tutorial with a dozen steps (6-12 minutes to completion) while a derivation of electric field with Gauss's law might require fifty or more steps (over 30 minutes to completion).

Students need to answer the "top level" question to receive credit on the homework problem but can answer the followup and tutorial questions if and when they need help. As students answer the different questions at each level in our homework system, we collect detiled information on when they click each submission button and what they answered. It is important to note that click statistics do not necessarily correspond to time spent on a homework problem. We cannot determine the exact amount of time that students spend on a particular homework problem (tutorials and all) by clicks alone. What if a student opened up a tutorial question and then left the computer to make a sandwich? What if a student re-opened a question before an exam to study? We can, however, analyze the quantity of clicks as a function of time as well as the pattern of clicks between homework problems to learn about how students are moving within and between homework problems.

Method

Once we collect the click information for students in a given semester, we bin the responses by a number of parameters including time, student, type of question answered, and whether it was a correct or incorrect answer. We experimented with a number of binning schemes and found that a bin size of 12 - 24 hours gave us good information on the general trends of the class while a bin size of two hours allowed us to see detailed patterns in the data without using too much computational power.

From this binned data, we can calculate a variety of descriptive statistics about student's usage of the system throughout the semester. Of course, these statistics are cross-referenced with other sources of data including class scores, online surveys, and focus group interview transcripts in order to paint a full picture of what is happening over the course of the semester.

Results and Discussion

Below are four animations that show the click statistics in the first unit of the fall 2015 semester of PHYS 24100 (i.e. approximately five weeks of the semester up until the first exam). Although this only represents a fraction of the total data, it highlights the general trends that we see in all units over both the spring and fall semester. We have divided up the clicks into twelve hour bins and used similar categories to those presented on the poster (open problem without answering a question, answer a top-level question, answer a followup question, and answer a tutorial question). The x-axis of each graph gives the problem number in order that they were presented during the semester. The y-axis gives the number of clicks on a problem.

A few key things to note about this data:

  1. The cycles of peaks described on the poster are very easy to see in the animations. Some students might view the problems before the assignment is due, but the vast majority of students focus on a homework assignment in the day or two beforehand. With an average assignment size of nine problems, this means that our average student is working through between three and nine problems per day, depending on when they start.
  2. The number of clicks where a student opened up a problem but did not answer any question was much higher than expected (about 1/3 of the aggregate click activity for the semester). We can identify where some of this activity is coming from (e.g. preparing for an exam, printing out the homework problems beforehand, opening a problem to enter an answer, etc.). However, much of the source of this click activity is still unknown.
  3. The optional followup questions were answered by some students in the first unit, but they represented a very small portion of the click activity per day. In general, students were skipping any part of the assignment that was not specifically related to their grade.
  4. There is a flurry of click activity before the first exam due to students studying past problems. Analysis shows that the tutorials are popular study tools before an exam.

Conclusions and Further Work

Some of the results that we are seeing above are what we would expect; most professors would agree that students tend to wait until the last minute to do their homework and students tend to focus on parts of the assignment that will earn then credit. However, other results are more surprising. Students are spending almost 1/3 of their clicks opening up homework problems, but answering no questions. Although some of these clicks are accounted for (as identified above), we are unsure why the number is so high.

More work is needed to identify the subtle click patterns that we see within the CITA system. At this point, we have completed our "aggregate" look at the click statistics in PHYS 24100. In the coming weeks we are going to dive into the clicks on a student by student basis as well as hold a series of focus group sessions to illuminate how students use CITA. We want to know how students traverse the system in order to build great tutorials for future generations of PHYS 24100.
